**HRIS software pricing** is one of the first criteria HR directors and SMB owners look at, even before comparing features. Vendor pricing grids, however, are rarely easy to read: per-employee billing, module packages, and setup fees that are not shown upfront. This article breaks down pricing models, the price ranges observed by company size, and the hidden costs to plan for before signing.

## How much does HRIS software cost: price ranges

The **HRIS** market in France is worth over 1.2 billion euros (Markess by Exaegis study, January 2025), driven by the widespread shift to the SaaS model. In this dominant model, billing is based on the number of employees per month, with a price that most often ranges between 2 and 15 euros per employee depending on the features included.

For a single module, such as time and schedule management or interview tracking, the lower range sits between 2 and 10 euros per employee per month. A full HR suite that includes payroll can instead exceed 30 to 70 euros per employee per month, depending on the level of support included in the contract.

### The special case of on-premise solutions

**On-premise** solutions, hosted on the company's own servers rather than in the cloud, remain a minority but still exist in some large organizations. Their operating cost generally sits between 1 and 3 euros per employee per month, plus an initial license investment often between 25,000 and 40,000 euros for the full installation.

## HRIS software pricing models

Three billing logics coexist on the market, each with different implications for the mid-term budget.

- **Per-employee SaaS subscription**: the most common model. The price automatically follows headcount changes, without renegotiating the contract at every hire.
- **Perpetual license + maintenance**: the historical model for on-premise solutions, with a high upfront investment followed by an annual fee of 15 to 20% of the license price.
- **Per-module or per-headcount-tier pricing**: many SaaS vendors offer tiers (up to 50, 200, 500 employees) rather than a strictly linear price, which can create threshold effects around a new hire.

The per-employee SaaS model dominates newly signed contracts by far, mainly because it requires neither installation costs nor infrastructure to maintain internally.

### Modules included in the published price

The base price shown on a vendor's website rarely covers the full scope of HR needs. **Payroll**, **advanced reporting** or **connectors** to other tools are often billed as add-ons, which explains the large gap between the headline rate advertised and the final invoice amount.

## HRIS software pricing by company size

The budget to plan for depends directly on the number of employees and the functional scope covered. The table below gives order-of-magnitude figures observed on the French market.

| Company size | Typical scope | Estimated monthly budget |
|---|---|---|
| Micro business (under 10 employees) | One module (time or reviews) | 50 to 150 euros |
| SMB (20 to 50 employees) | Time + talent, sometimes payroll | 200 to 1,000 euros |
| Mid-market (100 to 500 employees) | Full HR suite | 2,000 to 15,000 euros |
| Large enterprise (500+ employees) | Multi-site, multi-agreement HR suite | 15,000 euros and above |

These figures remain indicative: an industry with a high share of shift-based staff or several collective agreements pushes the price per employee up, regardless of company size.

## What makes HRIS software pricing vary

Several factors explain the price differences observed between vendors and between quotes, for the same number of employees.

- **Number of modules activated**: payroll, time tracking, talent and training are not systematically included in the base price.
- **Industry**: a scheduling engine capable of handling several collective agreements (retail, hospitality, healthcare, manufacturing) costs more than a generic HR core.
- **Level of support and onboarding**: dedicated assistance, team training and account management directly affect the rate.
- **Volume of data and integrations**: connectors to an external payroll tool, accounting software or time clocks add technical fees.
- **Contract length**: an annual contract is generally cheaper than a month-to-month subscription with no commitment.

## The hidden costs of an HRIS project

The price published by a vendor only reflects part of the real budget of an HRIS project. Three items are frequently underestimated during initial costing.

**Configuration** of payroll rules, collective agreements and review workflows is often the first unplanned expense: it takes internal time and sometimes a vendor service, billed on top of the subscription. **Data migration** from legacy tools or spreadsheets is the second item, with effort that grows with the age and volume of history to carry over.

Finally, **training** for HR teams and managers, along with change management for employees, determines how well the tool is actually adopted. Poorly onboarded HRIS software leads to low usage rates and a disappointing return on investment, regardless of its purchase price.

## How to budget and negotiate HRIS software

To build a realistic budget, it is recommended to ask vendors for a detailed quote that systematically includes the subscription price, setup fees, training and any connectors, rather than comparing only the headline monthly rate per employee shown on the website. Well-chosen [HRIS software](/en/blog/hris-software/) limits these hidden costs when its vendor includes onboarding support in the subscription.

Negotiation most often focuses on three levers: contract length, the number of modules bundled into a single contract, and the level of support included. A 12 to 24-month commitment frequently secures a 10 to 20% discount compared to the no-commitment monthly rate.
