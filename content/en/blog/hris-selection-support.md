**HRIS selection support** refers to the in-house or external guidance that structures the choice of HR software before a company commits to it for several years. Unlike a quick comparison of pricing grids, this process starts with an audit of real needs and follows a multi-step method through to the final recommendation.

Many French SMBs and mid-sized companies turn to this kind of support once managing HR on spreadsheets or an aging tool becomes a bottleneck. The goal is not only to find high-performing **HRIS software**, but to secure a choice that lastingly serves HR teams, managers and employees alike.

Certain signals suggest a company should structure a selection support process rather than improvise a quick comparison: a first HRIS project with no prior market experience, a scope spanning several sites or collective bargaining agreements, or a previous poorly scoped project that ended up with an underused tool. In these situations, the time invested in a structured process pays off well beyond the deployment phase.

## What an HRIS selection support engagement involves

An HRIS selection support engagement covers all the work that precedes signing a contract with a vendor: analyzing existing HR processes, formalizing needs, identifying candidate solutions, organizing demos and negotiating terms. It differs from a simple feature comparison through its methodical scope and its grounding in the company's real context.

This process can be run in two ways. In-house, it involves the HR director or an HR project lead, with occasional support from the IT department for technical aspects. Externally, a specialized firm brings its knowledge of the vendor market and a method already proven across dozens of similar projects. Our full definition of [HRIS software](/en/blog/hris-software/) details the functional building blocks these solutions usually cover.

### Why structure the process rather than compare vendors directly

Going straight to sales demos without a requirements document exposes a company to a classic bias: choosing the solution that made the best impression in a presentation, rather than the one that best answers priority needs. Structuring HRIS selection support upfront limits this risk and allows vendors to be compared against identical criteria.

## The key steps of an HRIS selection support process

An HRIS selection support process generally follows five steps, in an order that shapes the quality of the final decision.

### Auditing needs and existing HR processes

The first step maps current HR processes: schedule management, leave and absence tracking, payroll preparation, career development reviews, training tracking. This audit identifies the concrete pain points faced by HR teams and managers, an essential basis for prioritizing the features expected from the future HRIS.

### Drafting the requirements document

The requirements document translates identified needs into precise, ranked requirements, split between essential and secondary. It also defines the project scope: number of employees involved, applicable collective bargaining agreements, and integration constraints with existing tools such as a [payroll software](/en/blog/payroll-software-5-criteria/) already in place.

### Vendor benchmarking and demos

Based on the requirements document, a narrow panel of three to five relevant vendors is identified, then compared during demos built around the company's real use cases rather than generic presentations. SMBs weighing several generalist solutions can rely on our comparison of [HRIS software for small businesses](/en/blog/best-hris-software-small-business-2026/) to pre-qualify some candidates before this phase.

### Recommendation and reference checks

Following the demos, a supported recommendation is issued, backed by checking customer references at a comparable industry or company size. This step confirms that sales claims match real, long-term use of the software.

### Negotiation and contracting

The final step covers contractual terms: commitment length, data reversibility clauses, guaranteed service levels and deployment timeline. Support at this stage avoids unfavorable clauses that only surface when it is time to switch vendors.

Data reversibility deserves particular attention: it defines the format and timeframe in which the company recovers all of its HR data when a contract ends. This clause, often overlooked at signature, determines the company's freedom to switch HRIS solutions without losing its payroll and talent process history.

## Hiring a consulting firm or running the process in-house

The choice between external support and in-house leadership mainly depends on project size, available in-house time, and prior knowledge of the HRIS vendor market.

| Criterion | In-house leadership | External consulting firm |
|---|---|---|
| Vendor market knowledge | Limited, needs building | Already acquired across several projects |
| HR time required | High, over several months | Lower, the firm carries the workload |
| Objectivity toward vendors | Varies with internal history | Contractual neutrality |
| Direct cost | None beyond internal time | Dedicated engagement budget |
| Best suited for | Small company, simple project | Multi-site or first-ever HRIS project |

An SMB with fewer than fifty employees and a targeted need, such as leave management alone, can often run selection support in-house. Conversely, a multi-site group spanning several collective bargaining agreements, as covered in our [HRIS by industry](/en/blog/hris-by-industry-why-the-choice-depends-on-your-business/) report, benefits from securing the project through specialized external support.

## How much does HRIS selection support cost

The budget for external HRIS selection support varies widely with project scale. A targeted audit on a narrow scope, for example time tracking alone for a single site, generally costs a few thousand euros. A full engagement, including a detailed requirements document, benchmarking of several vendors, organized demos and support through deployment, can run into several tens of thousands of euros for a multi-site project spanning several collective bargaining agreements.

> "The cost of HRIS selection support remains marginal compared to the cost of a bad choice: a migration to redo, poor adoption by frontline teams, or critical features discovered missing after signature."
> — Shared observation from several HR consulting firms, 2026

## Mistakes to avoid in a supported HRIS selection

Certain mistakes come up repeatedly in HRIS selection support processes, whether run in-house or by an external provider.

- Starting demos before formalizing a requirements document, which favors the best sales presentation over actual fit with needs
- Skipping reference checks with customers in a comparable context to the company
- Underestimating the time needed for change management once the HRIS is selected, when manager adoption determines project success
- Choosing solely on price without factoring in total cost of ownership, including training, support and future upgrades
